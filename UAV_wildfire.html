Remote Sensing for Wildfire Research

NASA Ames FireSage Summer Internship

The FireSage program is a 10-week paid internship hosted by NASA Ames and San José State University's Wildfire Interdisciplinary Research Center, including involvement from the GeoFly Lab. Interns spend four weeks at NASA Ames and six weeks at SJSU, gaining immersive, hands-on experience in wildfire remote sensing, GIS programming, and ecological modeling. They explore tools like Landsat, MODIS, Sentinel, NAIP, Google Earth Engine, drones, and Python—working alongside NASA scientists, SJSU faculty, and industry partners such as Google, PG&E, and CalFire.

By working directly with the Geofly Lab, interns contribute to cutting-edge wildfire research and post-fire fuel management projects, developing practical skills in AI, data cleaning, and community resilience. The program emphasizes individual mentorship, interdisciplinary collaboration, and real-world applications in fire ecology, preparing students—particularly from underrepresented STEM fields—to tackle climate-driven challenges through innovative mapping and analysis techniques. For more details, visit the NASA FireSage program page: https://www.nasa.gov/firesage-program/.

California Canyon Fire Experiment

In October 2022, researchers from San José State University's Wildfire Interdisciplinary Research Center (WIRC) teamed up with CalFire to monitor the behavior of a prescribed fire in a steep canyon. The first experiment of its type and magnitude in the world, the California Canyon Fire Experiment involved the efforts of researchers from the University of Melbourne and Worcester Polytechnic Institute for data collection during the burn.

Under the supervision of Dr. Bo Yang, the GeoFly lab collected multiple data sets before, during, and after the experiment. Our goals were to monitor the immediate and long-term fire effects and to build protocols for the use of remote sensing to monitor fires. To this end, we collected 5cm multispectral imagery, 3cm RGB drone imagery, and thermal drone videos to use in conjunction with data from weather stations and thermal helicopter data. To this day, our data collection at this site is ongoing. Shrub point locations are being collected with high-accuracy GNSS to understand how temperatures are linked to biomass consumption, and drone data collection is also ongoing.

Primary data collection before and during the burn was led by student Owen Hussey. This includes multispectral and RGB maps, and 53 minutes of thermal video. See the Canyon Fire Experiment StoryMap for more information. The drone mapping data we collected can be viewed in this online 3D reconstruction.

Post-processing the thermal drone video involved manually georeferencing each individual keyframe, co-led by Xiangyu Ren. For each frame, 15 pairs of tie points were used to obtain a final full-motion video for use in ArcGIS alongside the pre- and post-fire drone maps. Using a land cover classification based on Anderson fuel types allowed us to observe significant temperature differences and associate those differences with different plant species.

Our fire effects monitoring efforts include a burn volume estimation using the digital surface model derived from drone imagery using Structure from Motion, also led by Xiangyu Ren. Due to a tilt in the imagery, additional ground control points were collected post-fire to match each orthomosaic’s elevation to the other. The post-fire DSM was then subtracted from the pre-fire DSM, resulting in a map showing the change in vegetation height, which allows us to estimate the burn volume after the fire, as well as the regrowth volume over time.

Pilot Study: Remote Sensing for the Home Ignition Zone

In recent years, there has been a dramatic increase in wildfire-related losses in California and elsewhere. There is consensus that suitable fuel removal and home hardening helps prevent losses, but we have observed low levels of compliance with NFPA, IBHS, and CalFire recommendations designed to reduce risk of loss.

In a 2023 pilot study led by co-PIs Dr. Kate Wilkin and Dr. Bo Yang with student lead Henri Brillon and Jack Jack Stevens, we explored how drone-based remote sensing and GIS can enrich, improve, and expedite home ignition zone (HIZ) assessments. By capturing photogrammetry data using drones, we produced high-resolution georeferenced orthomosaic maps and digital surface models of participating properties. Nine such drone surveys at properties in Truckee and Paradise were conducted for the pilot study. In cloud-based, open-access GIS, we are developing automated detection, measurement and identification scripts to indicate the spatial layout and priority level of individual fire hazards. We are using machine learning and deep learning models to democratize access to important fire safety and hazard detection technology.

This WIRC-IAB funded project presents the potential to improve safety recommendations to residents, to facilitate inspections, and to provide better data to insurers. More data collection (N=50) is slated for the summer of 2024.

Prescribed Fire Mapping

In collaboration with the Wilkin Fire Ecology Lab and CalFire, we have produced drone mapping and high-accuracy GNSS products for prescribed fires in the Santa Lucia Preserve and at Wilder Ranch State Park. Vegetation monitoring at both sites focuses on Coyote Brush, an aggressive native plant whose presence land managers would benefit from reducing. By mapping these burn areas and tagging plot locations and feature perimeters with GNSS, we are helping biologists create a clearer picture of fire effects and refine their predictions for which fuel treatments to recommend to land managers.

Post-Fire Vegetation Response at San Vicente Redwoods

The San Vicente Redwoods are crossed by Warrenella Road, an eight-mile dirt road on private land. This patch of forest burned during the 2020 CZU Complex Fire and has since been identified as a suitable location for a shaded fuel break.

SJSU Biology researchers led by Dr. Julia Gaudinski and Dr. Kate Wilkin are examining the post-fire response of this area through plant and soils analyses. Meanwhile, Dr. Bo Yang’s GeoFly Lab has been conducting drone mapping efforts at this site using multispectral, RGB, and LiDAR sensors with student participants Henri Brillon and William Lee. This allows us to quantify site-wide vegetation response to fire and to fuel treatments, and subsequent recovery. In addition to helping with drone mapping, student lead Melina Kompella has also been conducting in-situ spectroscopy measurements to assess vegetation response to these fuel treatments. Together, these data sets will allow investigators to form a clear picture of vegetation and soils response to fire and to fuel treatments, and create models to help land managers understand where and how to implement them in the future.
