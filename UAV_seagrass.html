UAV Seagrass Mapping for Coastal Conservation

Integrating UAV remote sensing, ecological analysis, and interdisciplinary collaboration

The UAV mapping of coastal seagrass sites along the Pacific (West) coast of North America is being undertaken through a $1.3 million NSF collaborative grant co-led with Dr. Timothy Hawthorne. This project represents one of the earliest attempts to employ UAV mapping in coastal management and seagrass conservation, assessing three major stressors—climate warming, altered biodiversity, and disease—on seagrass health through ecological, microbiological, computational, geospatial analysis, and UAV remote sensing.

Wasting disease causes eelgrass to lose greenness, detected via low-altitude UAV imagery and fine-scale image analysis. By leveraging UAVs’ temporal flexibility to capture low-tide conditions and broader coverage, and validating remotely sensed data with in situ samples processed by the EeLISA tool and AI-based blade scans, we demonstrate that Green Leaf Area Index (G-LAI) explains up to 58% of disease variation. High-resolution (<5 cm) orthomosaics enable rapid disease detection and monitoring.

Interdisciplinary collaborations include Smithsonian MarineGeo, Cornell University, UC Davis, University of Alaska Fairbanks, Oregon State University, San Diego State University, and Hakai Institute. Key collaborators: Carla Gomes, Deanna Beatty, Drew Harvell, Emmett Duffy, and many others across institutions.

Field sites span from Alaska to southern California, including Prince of Wales Island, BC’s central coast, San Juan Islands, Yaquina Bay, Coos Bay, Bodega & Tomales Bays, and Mission Bay. Over 10,000 drone images were collected to quantify eelgrass coverage, disease prevalence, and environmental gradients.

We maintain an Open-access Drone Mapping Course covering flight planning, FAA Part 107, image stitching, GIS analysis, and safety guidelines for coastal mapping.
